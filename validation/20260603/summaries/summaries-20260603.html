Long

Launching the Carrying the Fire series — a phrase borrowed from Cormac McCarthy, where the fire is the truth and the Word that lights your path — the pastor began on the road to Emmaus in Luke 24:30-32. The disciples recognized the risen Jesus only as he broke the bread, then asked each other, "Were not our hearts burning within us while he talked with us on the road and opened the scriptures to us?" That burning, he said, is the feeling the whole series is after. The Bereans modeled it too: Acts 17:11 calls them noble in character because they received the message with eagerness and examined the Scriptures every day.

Scripture is also a weapon. When the devil tempted Jesus three times in the wilderness, Jesus answered every time by quoting Scripture (Matthew 4:1-11) — and if it's good enough for Jesus, it's good enough for me. The enemy's primary weapon is lies: that you are hopeless, that you cannot grow, that others will never change. The pastor pointed to the Armor of God in Ephesians 6:10-18, where belt, breastplate, shield, and helmet all culminate in the one offensive piece, the sword of the Spirit, which is the word of God. The Word also trains discernment for life's biggest decisions — whom to marry, where to move. God's instruction may contradict your common sense, your desires, even your parents, yet it will never contradict His written Word, so studying Scripture strengthens your ability to recognize His voice.

The talk closed with the series' central Hebrew discovery. Deuteronomy 6:4, the Shema, is the foundational declaration of Judaism: "Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one." Jesus opened with that very line when asked for the greatest commandment, then gave two — love God, love people (Mark 12:29-31). The Hebrew word for one, echad, signals a unified oneness, and it reappears in Genesis 2:24, where man and wife become one flesh. What is bound on earth is bound in heaven (Matthew 16:19), so the study of the Word aims at unbreakable faith and unbreakable families.
