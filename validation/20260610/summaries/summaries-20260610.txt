Long

The pastor opens by recapping the foundation of the Carry the Fire series: the Hebrew word echad describes both the oneness of God in Deuteronomy 6:4 and the one-flesh union of husband and wife in Genesis 2:24, so God's oneness is a harmony of two becoming one. That harmony has practical weight — unity between husband and wife creates the bedrock where children first learn love, becoming an instrument through which God pours love into a home. From there the pastor connects two threads running through Scripture: God is spirit, hovering over the waters in Genesis 1:2, and God is love, as 1 John 4:8 declares. The two grow together. The more we grow spiritually, the more capacity we have to love, because God wants all our spirituality aimed at increasing the strength to love.

Ephesians 4:18 names our separation from God as a hardening of the heart, and Ezekiel 36:26 carries God's promise to remove the heart of stone and give a soft, warm heart of flesh. Jesus' parable of the least of these (Matthew 25:40) shows that loving and serving any person counts spiritually, whether or not we know whom we are serving. Returning to the fall, the pastor observes that Satan told Eve a kind of truth about physical death while God spoke of spiritual death — Satan's signature move is telling truths and then twisting the call to action. Adam responded with fig leaves, fear, and blame, yet no amount of shame could reconnect him to God; shame has never been part of God's restorative process. After Genesis 3:24, the whole Bible becomes a story of reconnection — the warming of the human heart back toward God.

Paul crystallizes this in 1 Corinthians 13:1-3: tongues, prophecy, mountain-moving faith, and radical generosity gain nothing without love. Jesus distills everything into the greatest commandment of Mark 12:29-31 — love God with all your heart, soul, mind, and strength, and love your neighbor as yourself — and the eternal life he promises in John 10:27-28 means harmony with God himself. The pastor closes honestly, naming his own blind spots in forgiving people in authority, and invites everyone into a daily, tangible walk of reconnection.
