Long

The pastor opens by recapping the heart of the Carry the Fire series: the oneness of God is a harmony, since the Hebrew word echad describes both the Lord being one in Deuteronomy 6:4 and a man and wife becoming one flesh in Genesis 2:24. Unity between husband and wife, he says, builds the bedrock where children first learn love. From there he connects two threads running through Scripture — God is spirit, hovering over the waters in Genesis 1:2, and God is love in 1 John 4:8 — and draws his central claim: every spiritual practice exists to increase our capacity to love. The separation we feel from God is a hardening of the heart (Ephesians 4:18), and God promises to trade the heart of stone for a soft, warm heart of flesh (Ezekiel 36:26). Jesus' parable of the least of these (Matthew 25:40) proves the point: loving and serving any person grows you spiritually, whether or not you know whom you are serving.

The pastor then rereads the fall. Satan deceived Eve by telling a truth — she would not die physically — while twisting the call to action toward disobedience, a tactic he still uses today. Even after the fruit, God came looking for an open-hearted conversation, but Adam hid, covered himself, and shifted blame. Shame, the pastor stresses, has never been part of God's restorative process; no amount of it reconnects a heart to God. After Genesis 3:24, the entire Bible becomes one long story of reconnection.

That reconnection runs on love. In 1 Corinthians 13:1-3, tongues, prophecy, mountain-moving faith, and radical generosity gain nothing without it. Jesus distills everything into the greatest commandment — love God with all your heart, soul, mind, and strength, and love your neighbor as yourself (Mark 12:29-31) — and the pastor frames eternal life (John 10:27-28) as harmony with God himself. He closes by inviting everyone to examine their blind spots, especially the hard work of forgiving people in authority, and to walk with Jesus in a daily, tangible way.
